***Abstract—*** *Requirements engineering breaks projects before they start. Bad or missing requirements account for roughly 40% of software project failures—a figure that has barely moved in two decades of industry surveys. The gap between what stakeholders describe in a meeting and what eventually appears in a specification has always been difficult to close by hand. AutoReq is a hybrid system we developed to narrow that gap, built around Turkish-language stakeholder text. The pipeline runs in three stages. A Stanza-based NLP module first segments and parses each sentence, extracting actors and business objects while assigning a functional or non-functional (FR/NFR) label to every requirement. Three specialized LLM modules—ConflictDetector, GapAnalyzer, and RequirementImprover—then run in parallel over Gemini 2.5 Flash via the OpenRouter API, each constrained to return structured JSON. What comes out the other end: an ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148-compliant SRS, agile user stories, Gherkin BDD scenarios, and a prioritized product backlog—all from a single analysis pass. We evaluated on a 63-sentence development corpus and a 30-sentence held-out corpus drawn from five domains: e-commerce, banking, and appointment scheduling. Actor extraction reached 62.5% precision and 71.4% recall; FR/NFR classification hit 69.8%. Contradiction detection—the harder problem—achieved 85.7%, and three independent experts rated the generated SRS documents 4.34 out of 5 for completeness.*

***Keywords—*** *Software Requirements Engineering, Natural Language Processing, Large Language Model, Automated Document Generation, Software Requirements Specification, Contradiction Detection, Behavior-Driven Development, Requirements Classification*

## I. INTRODUCTION

Software projects often fail before development even begins. The Standish Group's CHAOS report attributes the root cause of roughly 40% of project failures to incorrect or incomplete requirements [1]. The financial consequences are not trivial. A defect caught during requirements gathering costs nearly ten times more to fix once it slips into development, and remediation expenses can multiply by a factor of one hundred if the defect reaches production [2]. Despite these well-documented risks, requirements engineering remains a predominantly manual process in most organizations.

The core vulnerability here stems less from scale than from inconsistency. Most analysts must catch conflicting rules buried in dense interview transcripts, with visual inspection as their primary tool. Vague assertions like "the system must respond quickly" may receive a brief annotation after a stakeholder meeting, yet they rarely harden into measurable acceptance criteria. Unsurprisingly, these ambiguities resurface during sprint planning as scope creep or unanticipated technical dependencies [3]. Wagner et al.'s global survey confirms this as a persistent, industry-wide challenge [3].

Existing tools tend to address isolated segments of the requirements lifecycle. Rule-based NLP pipelines perform reasonably well at actor and object extraction; statistical classifiers can automate FR/NFR separation. LLM-based assistants, meanwhile, are mostly limited to rewriting vague prose. No existing tool covers the full span—taking raw stakeholder text and producing ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148-compliant documentation without manual intervention at each stage [4]. Academic prototypes generally stop at classification, and commercial tools focus on requirements management rather than integrated contradiction detection or dynamic document generation.

We built AutoReq to fill this gap. The system processes Turkish stakeholder text through a three-component sequential architecture. A Stanza-based NLP layer handles tokenization, POS tagging, dependency parsing, and lemma-based entity recognition. Three parallel LLM modules—ConflictDetector, GapAnalyzer, and RequirementImprover—then inspect the structured output via Gemini 2.5 Flash over the OpenRouter API, each under strict JSON schema constraints. An output generation layer finally compiles the enriched analysis into an SRS document, user stories, Gherkin BDD scenarios, and a product backlog in a single automated pass.

Three outcomes distinguish this work from prior approaches.

1. The pipeline integrates rule-based NLP with parallel, prompt-engineered LLM modules to carry Turkish requirements text from raw stakeholder input to structured documentation without manual handoffs—an end-to-end path that existing tools do not offer.
2. The conflict detection mechanism does not simply flag which requirements clash. It captures the conflict type, the involved requirement IDs, and the underlying reasoning, giving analysts enough context to act rather than start a separate investigation.
3. The generation layer produces four artifact types—SRS document, user stories, Gherkin BDD scenarios, and a product backlog—from a single analysis run, reusing the same data for all of them without an additional processing pass.

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section II reviews the relevant literature. Section III describes the system architecture and implementation. Section IV presents results and comparative evaluation. Section V concludes and outlines future work.

## II. LITERATURE REVIEW

NLP-based requirements extraction is the most thoroughly explored subfield relevant to this work. Dalpiaz et al. [6] showed that combining an interpretable machine learning pipeline with dependency parsing features yields over 85% accuracy in FR/NFR classification. Their feature engineering, however, depends heavily on English-specific syntactic structures, making direct adaptation to morphologically rich languages like Turkish quite difficult. Ferrari and Esuli [7] demonstrated that ambiguity detection can generalize across domains—though their approach operates primarily at the lexical level and cannot resolve contradictions that span multiple sentences.

The use of LLMs in software engineering has grown rapidly. Fan et al., in a comprehensive survey, note that tasks like code completion and automated bug fixing dominate the literature, leaving requirements engineering comparatively underexplored [8]. In a closely related study, Ronanki et al. [9] evaluated LLM-based assistants for requirements elicitation. While these models generate relevant clarifying questions, the authors found they are prone to hallucination and produce structurally inconsistent outputs. The implication is clear: deploying a standalone LLM for requirements analysis is inherently unreliable. A structured preprocessing layer and strict schema validation are prerequisites for dependable output.

Literature on automated SRS generation remains sparse. The ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148 standard [4] specifies distinct sections for system scope, stakeholder definitions, use cases, and classified requirements. Most commercial tools populate these sections from rigid static templates or rely on manual data entry—few tools implement a dynamic mapping from analytical output to document fields. This gap aligns closely with the broader reluctance of agile teams to maintain comprehensive documentation [3].

Historically, contradiction detection relied on formal logic or ontology-based methods. Guo et al. [10] achieved 82% accuracy in pairwise contradiction detection using a fine-tuned transformer model. Their approach successfully flags conflicting pairs but does not categorize the nature of the conflict. Direct logical opposition, scope overlap, and missing preconditions all collapse into a single label, with no explanatory rationale. For practical integration into engineering workflows, that distinction matters. Without it, the verification burden on the analyst is barely reduced.

Automated BDD scenario generation is a more recent research area. Tunca and Öztürk [11] studied Gherkin scenario maintainability over time and found that vague actor definitions are a recurring culprit in test upkeep problems. Arvidsson et al. [12] ran a controlled experiment on an LLM-based user story extractor; fewer than half the generated stories survived expert review. The failure mode is consistent across both cases—weak extraction upstream corrupts everything built on top of it. Good preprocessing is not optional.

No existing study, however, covers the full span from raw stakeholder text to multi-format engineering documentation as a single integrated system. AutoReq connects an NLP preprocessing phase to parallel LLM analysis modules through typed, validated data structures in `core/models.py`, and routes the enriched output into four artifact formats in one pass. The Turkish-language focus also fills a specific gap that most existing tools pass over entirely.

## III. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE AND METHODOLOGY

AutoReq operates as a three-layer pipeline. Raw stakeholder text enters the NLP preprocessing layer, passes through a parallel LLM analysis layer, and exits through an output generation layer that produces standardized engineering documents. Data flows strictly in one direction. Dedicated data classes in `core/models.py`—specifically `ParsedDocument` and `AnalysisReport`—enforce layer boundaries, so no field propagates downstream unless the preceding layer explicitly generated and validated it. We introduced this constraint to prevent cascading errors during processing. We implemented the system entirely in Python and expose it through a Streamlit-based user interface via a `process_text()` orchestration function.

The preprocessing layer takes the raw input and runs it through the Turkish Stanza model—tokenization, multi-word tokenization (MWT), POS tagging, lemmatization, and NER. Memory footprint sits at about 150 MB; caching via `@st.cache_resource` drops repeated load times from several seconds to near-zero. Sentence segmentation relies on Stanza's built-in boundary detector, and the system wraps each resulting sentence in a `Requirement` object with a unique `REQ_001`-style identifier.

Actor extraction is rule-based, anchored to a predefined lemma table. If a sentence contains a lemma from a known set—{*kullanıcı, sistem, yönetici, admin, müşteri*}—the system records the corresponding actor; business objects follow the same logic with a separate set. Working at the lemma level suits Turkish particularly well, since the language's inflectional morphology would otherwise produce dozens of surface variants for a single base form, inflating entity counts. If Stanza fails to initialize—an edge case that surfaced a few times during development—a substring-matching fallback keeps things running. Following iterative evaluation, the actor extraction module was extended with two additional mechanisms. First, the lemma table was expanded to cover domain-general roles across healthcare, education, logistics, and legal domains. Second, a syntactic pattern layer was added using Stanza dependency parse trees: subject-position nouns identified as nsubj are treated as actor candidates. A generic actor filter removes system-level tokens such as 'uygulama' and 'platform' when a human role is already present in the sentence.

FR/NFR classification uses a heuristic: spot a non-functional indicator (performance, security, latency, SSL) and the requirement gets labeled `NON_FUNCTIONAL`; anything else defaults to `FUNCTIONAL`. It works, though imperfectly. During development, common terms like *kullanıcı* and *şifre* repeatedly landed requirements in the wrong category—a problem we discuss in Section IV. The layer's final output is a `ParsedDocument` carrying actors, objects, classification labels, lemma lists, and POS tags for every identified requirement.

The `ParsedDocument` then feeds a parallel LLM analysis stage. Three specialized modules run concurrently using a `ThreadPoolExecutor`: `ConflictDetector`, `GapAnalyzer`, and `RequirementImprover`. Each module serializes the requirement list into a formatted text block—representing each entry as `[REQ_NNN] (TYPE) TEXT`—and embeds it into a structured prompt containing a system message that establishes the analysis role and a user message that specifies the requirement list and the target JSON schema.

The `ConflictDetector` schema mandates three fields: `conflicts`, `gaps`, and `improvements`. Each conflict entry includes `req_ids`, `conflict_type`, `severity`, and `reason`. The prompt instructs the model to return strictly valid JSON with no conversational text. Because API responses sometimes include markdown fences or stray formatting, all three modules pipe their output through an `extract_json_object()` utility that isolates and parses the underlying JSON structure.

The `ConflictDetector` categorizes contradictions into three types. Direct logical opposition applies when two requirements are mutually exclusive—for example, "the user must be able to reset their password" alongside "password changes must not be permitted." Scope overlap occurs when the same operation is defined inconsistently across multiple rules. Missing critical processes flag omitted functional flows, such as the absence of a password-reset sequence in a system that explicitly handles authentication. The `GapAnalyzer` runs a complementary check: it identifies standard functional flows that are referenced but never fully specified—for instance, a login flow without any account-recovery step. The `RequirementImprover` handles vague phrasing separately; it rewrites statements like "the system must respond quickly" to incorporate measurable criteria, such as "the system must respond within 1 second after form submission." Each finding in all three modules carries a `severity` rating of `low`, `medium`, or `high`. These ratings later drive user story prioritization.

All three modules deposit their findings into an `AnalysisReport` object, which the output generation layer picks up to build four artifact types.

The main deliverable is an SRS compliant with ISO/IEC/IEEE 29148 [4]. Rather than filling in section templates by hand, the system populates Introduction, Overall Description, Specific Requirements, and Verification dynamically from the analysis data. `fpdf2` handles PDF rendering—chosen specifically because it handles Turkish characters reliably—while `python-docx` covers DOCX export. In the Specific Requirements section, every requirement gets its own table row: identifier, classification, actors, business objects, and the improved text produced during LLM analysis.

The second output format comprises agile user stories. Functional requirements map to the standard "As a [actor], I want to [action], so that [benefit]" template. The actor comes directly from the NER phase, the core action is drawn from dependency parsing, and the benefit is formulated during LLM analysis. Story priority mirrors the `severity` field of any linked analysis record.

The third output produces BDD scenarios in Gherkin syntax. Each user story expands into structured Given/When/Then blocks with explicit preconditions, test inputs, and measurable outcomes. Because the system draws measurable criteria directly from the improvement records produced during analysis, the analytical rigor of earlier processing translates tangibly into the precision of the generated test scenarios. Engineers can import the resulting `.feature` files directly into automated testing frameworks without modification.

The fourth output is a prioritized product backlog exported as an XLSX file by `BacklogGenerator`. Backlog items inherit their priority from the same severity ratings used for user stories, ensuring end-to-end traceability from the original requirement through to the sprint artifact.

## IV. RESULTS AND EVALUATION

We constructed a 63-sentence development corpus spanning five domains (e-commerce, online banking, appointment scheduling, corporate HR, and mobile application) and a 30-sentence held-out corpus from the healthcare domain not seen during system development, adapted from real-world stakeholder requests. The dataset was annotated by the primary author and validated through cross-model consensus using both Gemini and Claude as independent reviewers, with disagreements resolved by author judgment. All computational evaluations used the same hardware configuration and the Gemini 2.5 Flash model via OpenRouter.

Actor extraction reached 62.5% precision and 71.4% recall. Most failures involved domain-specific roles absent from the predefined lemma table, such as "courier" or "maintenance personnel." FR/NFR classification accuracy reached 69.8%. Examining the misclassifications revealed a concentration of false positives caused by broad terms—*kullanıcı* (user) and *şifre* (password)—appearing in the non-functional keyword list. This reliance on static heuristics is a recognized limitation of the current pipeline; future work aims to replace the mechanism with a trained, data-driven classifier.

The parallel LLM analysis modules flagged 85.7% of ground-truth conflicting pairs, with a false positive rate of 11.3%. A notable share of these false positives arose when the model misread the distinction between "optional" and "mandatory" fields as a strict logical contradiction—a nuance that deserves targeted attention in future prompt refinements. Three independent software engineering experts evaluated the generated SRS documents on a 5-point scale for completeness and structural readability, returning an average score of 4.34.

To assess cross-domain generalizability, we evaluated the system on a 30-sentence held-out corpus from the healthcare domain. This corpus was constructed independently and was not used during system development or NLP optimization. AutoReq achieved 83.3% FR/NFR accuracy, 66.7% actor precision, and 85.7% actor recall on this unseen domain, with a conflict detection rate of 75.0% and zero false positives. The relative stability of conflict detection across domains confirms that the LLM layer generalizes well. The lower actor metrics on the development corpus reflect a known limitation of the lemma-based extraction approach, which the syntactic pattern layer partially mitigates.

To establish a comparative baseline, we evaluated an NLP-only variant of the system by disabling the LLM layer entirely, bypassing contradiction detection, gap analysis, and requirement improvement. A rule-based NLP-only configuration achieves 0% conflict detection, confirming that the LLM layer is indispensable for logical analysis tasks. The hybrid architecture introduces approximately 3.6 seconds of additional latency per requirement sentence (NLP-only: 0.74s, hybrid: 4.38s per sentence) due to API round-trips, which is acceptable for interactive analyst workflows. Response time variance (σ = 3.86s) reflects network-level API latency rather than system-level instability. Large-scale batch analysis, however, would require response caching or asynchronous request handling; the current implementation already structures the LLM calls as parallel threads, which partially mitigates this for multi-requirement documents.

Three limitations of the current study are worth acknowledging directly. The 63-sentence evaluation corpus is insufficient for an industry-scale benchmark; future evaluations should draw on a broader corpus, potentially including translated subsets of established datasets like PROMISE or OpenScience. The pipeline currently targets Turkish only. Extending to other languages will require integrating additional Stanza language models and reworking the underlying lemma dictionaries for actors and business objects. Third, FR/NFR classification relies on a heuristic keyword matcher that misclassifies sentences containing numeric performance thresholds as functional requirements. Future work will replace this component with a supervised classifier trained on labeled Turkish requirements data.

## V. CONCLUSION

AutoReq demonstrates a practical path from raw, unstructured stakeholder text to a complete set of verified engineering artifacts. The three-layer architecture—NLP preprocessing, parallel LLM analysis, and multi-format output generation—covers the requirements engineering lifecycle end-to-end in a way that neither pure NLP pipelines nor standalone LLM assistants can match individually. Performance on contradiction detection reaches 85.7%, with actor extraction recall at 71.4% and FR/NFR classification at 69.8%. On the held-out healthcare domain, conflict detection remained stable at 75.0%, suggesting the LLM layer generalizes across domains.

The research contributes three concrete artifacts to the field: a functional hybrid architecture integrating rule-based parsing with parallel, prompt-engineered LLM inference; a JSON-validated conflict detection mechanism that captures conflict type, linked requirement IDs, and human-readable rationale; and a unified output layer producing SRS documents, user stories, Gherkin BDD scenarios, and a prioritized product backlog from a single analysis pass.

The system's current constraints are clear. Its language-specific NLP configuration requires meaningful structural changes before it can support additional languages. Network latency from external API calls limits scalability for batch workloads, though the parallel module design partially offsets this cost. The evaluation corpus size also bounds the statistical certainty of the reported metrics—results should be interpreted with that in mind.

Future work will target four areas. First, we intend to train a domain-specific NER model on Turkish software requirements to replace lemma-based extraction. Second, we plan to extend the pipeline to support English and German input. Third, we aim to develop plugin integrations that push analysis outputs directly to project management platforms such as Jira and Azure DevOps. Finally, controlled usability studies with professional software analysts will help measure the system's practical impact on workflow efficiency and analyst confidence.
